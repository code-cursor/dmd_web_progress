Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)

DMD is a progressive genetic disorder causing muscle degeneration and weakness. It primarily affects boys due to a mutation in the dystrophin gene, leading to a lack of dystrophin protein essential for muscle function. Symptoms often appear in early childhood and may affect heart and breathing muscles, reducing life expectancy. While there is no cure, treatments like steroids, physical therapy, and cardiac/respiratory support can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life.

Causes

  • DMD is caused by a mutation in the gene on the X chromosome that makes the protein dystrophin.
  • Without enough functional dystrophin, muscle fibers break down and are replaced by fat and fibrous tissue.
  • The disorder can be inherited or occur spontaneously in individuals with no family history.

Symptoms

Early signs (around age 6) include muscle weakness, developmental delays, and fatigue.
Difficulty running, climbing stairs, or jumping.
Frequent falls.
Increased calf size (pseudohypertrophy).
Waddling gait, using hands to “walk” up legs to stand.

  • Loss of ability to walk (often between ages 9–11)
  • Curvature of the spine (scoliosis)
  • Weakening of heart and breathing muscles

Management & Treatment

Medications: Corticosteroids to reduce inflammation and slow muscle loss. Eteplirsen may help produce some dystrophin.
Physical therapy: Maintain muscle strength and mobility. Range-of-motion exercises prevent joint contractures.
Medical devices: Breathing assistance (ventilator), braces, or surgery for scoliosis/joint issues.
Cardiac care: Regular monitoring and medications for heart-related complications.
