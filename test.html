Treatment Options for DMD

Current treatments for Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) target inflammation, help produce dystrophin, or address the disease at a genetic level. Always consult your healthcare provider to determine the best treatment plan.

aGamree¬Æ (vamorolone)
  • Function: Reduces inflammation by blocking proteins causing inflammation.
  • Ages: 2 years and older.
  • Administration: Oral suspension, taken once daily with meals.
  • Side effects: Cushingoid features, psychiatric issues, weight gain, increased infection risk, etc.
Amondys 45 (casimersen)
  • Function: Exon skipping therapy to make a shorter form of the dystrophin protein.
  • Ages: All ages with the right genetic mutation.
  • Administration: IV infusion, potentially with a port.
  • Side effects: Respiratory infections, joint pain, fever.
Elevidys (delandistrogene moxeparvovec-rokl)
  • Function: Gene therapy to introduce mini-dystrophin.
  • Ages: Eligibility criteria listed on Elevidys website.
  • Administration: Single-dose IV infusion.
  • Side effects: Liver injury, myositis, myocarditis.
Emflaza‚Ñ¢ (deflazacourt)
  • Function: Corticosteroid to reduce inflammation.
  • Ages: 2 years and older.
  • Administration: Oral tablets or suspension, with or without food.
  • Side effects: Weight gain, skin redness, abdominal discomfort.
Exondys 51 (eteplirsen)
  • Function: Exon skipping therapy for dystrophin production.
  • Ages: Any age with the right genetic mutation.
  • Administration: Weekly IV infusion.
  • Side effects: Allergic reactions, chest pain, wheezing.
Prednisone (prednisolone)
  • Function: Corticosteroid for inflammation.
  • Ages: Consult with your doctor.
  • Administration: Oral, with food.
  • Side effects: Sleep problems, acne, weight gain, slow wound healing.
Viltepso¬Æ (viltolarsen)
  • Function: Exon skipping to help produce dystrophin.
  • Ages: Any age with the right mutation.
  • Administration: Weekly IV infusion.
  • Side effects: Respiratory infections, injection site reactions, cough.
Vyondys 53 (golodirsen)
  • Function: Exon skipping therapy to decrease muscle weakness and prolong motor function.
  • Ages: Any age with the right genetic mutation.
  • Administration: Weekly IV infusion.
  • Side effects: Allergic reactions, rash, fever.
Key Points
  • These treatments vary in how they work ‚Äî exon skipping, gene therapy, or corticosteroids.
  • Most treatments are administered via oral or IV infusion.
  • Side effects range from mild (weight gain, cough) to severe (liver injury, allergic reactions).
  • Patient assistance programs help families access and afford treatments.

Always consult your healthcare provider before starting any treatment.

Understanding Therapy in Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD)

A guide to physiotherapy and occupational therapy designed to help people with DMD stay active, comfortable, and independent.

üí¨ Overview

People with DMD benefit from regular physiotherapy and occupational therapy to stay active and maintain independence. These therapies aim to keep muscles flexible, improve posture, and make daily tasks easier.

üß© Physiotherapy

  • Maintains muscle strength and flexibility
  • Reduces risk of joint tightness and contractures
  • Encourages safe activity and stretching
  • Supports recovery after illness or surgery

üõãÔ∏è Occupational Therapy

  • Makes everyday tasks easier and safer
  • Recommends helpful aids and home adjustments
  • Provides adaptive seating, splints, and mobility tools
  • Supports school participation and learning

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è The Five Stages of DMD Care

  • Pre-Ambulant / Early Diagnosis (Green): Focus on play, stretching, and preventing joint tightness.
  • Early Ambulant (Yellow): Support walking, balance, and conserve energy.
  • Late Ambulant (Orange): Prevent falls and prepare for mobility aids.
  • Early Non-Ambulant (Purple): Maintain posture, breathing, and wheelchair comfort.
  • Late Non-Ambulant (Blue): Focus on comfort, respiratory health, and daily independence.

üí® Breathing & Bone Health

If breathing test results drop, regular specialist care is essential. DMD also increases the risk of weak bones (osteoporosis), especially when using corticosteroids.

See the Bone Health Family Guide for detailed advice.

ü¶¥ Managing Fractures & Scoliosis

  • Spine Fractures: Report any back pain or tenderness to your team.
  • Long Bone Fractures: Contact your neuromuscular team immediately if you break a bone.
  • Scoliosis: Keep good posture and monitor your spine regularly with specialists.

üö∂‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Mobility & Wheelchairs

Early use of a buggy or manual wheelchair may help manage fatigue. As DMD progresses, powered chairs support independence and posture.

Chairs should be custom-fitted to ensure comfort, balance, and skin protection.

üîÑ Transition & Ongoing Support

  • Transfer Stage: The shift from walking to non-walking usually lasts around 1.5 years and needs special support.
  • Transition to Adult Care: Start planning early in your teenage years with your therapy team.
  • Regular Appointments: Schedule at least every 6 months or as advised.

üß† Behaviour, Learning & Mental Health

Some individuals with DMD may have learning or behavioural challenges. Therapists adapt sessions, use parent input, and support mental health by building confidence and social skills.

Families are encouraged to connect with other support groups for shared learning and emotional strength.
